The House on Buzzards Bay

by Dwyer Murphy

Buzz Meter:

A gripping mystery that blends coastal gothic atmosphere with modern noir sensibilities. Murphy delivers a haunting tale of family secrets and the ghosts that won't stay buried.

Reviewed on June 15, 2023 By Sarah Johnson Mystery, Gothic

Dwyer Murphy's "The House on Buzzards Bay" is a masterclass in atmospheric storytelling, weaving together elements of classic gothic fiction with a sharp, contemporary edge. The novel opens with attorney Caroline Byrne returning to her family's crumbling estate on the Massachusetts coast after receiving a cryptic letter from her estranged uncle.

Murphy's prose is both lyrical and precise, perfectly capturing the eerie beauty of the coastal setting. The house itself emerges as a central character - its creaking floorboards, hidden passages, and ocean views that seem to watch as much as they are watched. The author builds tension slowly but relentlessly, layering family secrets with each chapter.

What sets this mystery apart is Murphy's attention to character development. Caroline is no stock detective - she's a complex protagonist wrestling with her own demons even as she uncovers her family's dark history. The supporting cast, from the enigmatic groundskeeper to the town historian with her own agenda, are equally well-drawn.

The plot takes several unexpected turns, though some readers might find the final revelation slightly rushed. Nevertheless, the journey to that conclusion is so richly atmospheric and psychologically compelling that most will forgive this minor flaw. Murphy's background as a lawyer brings authenticity to the legal subplot that runs through the narrative.

"The House on Buzzards Bay" will appeal to fans of both classic gothic fiction and modern literary mysteries. It's a perfect read for stormy nights, though you might find yourself glancing over your shoulder more than usual afterward.

College Student Perspective

This novel offers excellent material for literature students examining modern gothic tropes or the evolution of mystery fiction. The layered narrative structure and unreliable narrator would make for compelling classroom discussion. Psychology students might also analyze the family dynamics and trauma themes throughout the book.

About the Author

Dwyer Murphy is a former attorney and editor at the Paris Review. His work has appeared in numerous literary journals, and "The House on Buzzards Bay" is his second novel. Murphy's writing is known for its atmospheric settings and complex character studies. He currently lives in Brooklyn but spends summers on Cape Cod, the inspiration for much of his coastal fiction.

Murphy will be speaking at several college campuses this fall as part of the "New Voices in Mystery Fiction" tour.
